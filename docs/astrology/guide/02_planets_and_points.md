## The one-line version

The **planets** are the actors, the *what* of the chart: a set of basic human
drives and functions. Signs style them ([chapter 01](./01_the_zodiac.md)), houses
locate them ([chapter 03](./03_houses.md)), aspects connect them
([chapter 04](./04_aspects.md)). Everything else is scenery; the planets are the
cast.

## The key that organizes all of them: speed

Before any individual meaning, learn this one principle, because it makes the
whole crowd of bodies fall into order: **the faster a body moves, the more
personal it is; the slower it moves, the more collective.** The Moon races
through the zodiac at about 14° a day, so it's uniquely yours. Pluto crawls at
about 0.01° a day, so it's shared by everyone born across two decades. In Jung's
chart the Moon moves **14.2° per day** and Pluto moves **0.01°**, a ratio of more
than a thousand to one. That single spread sorts the planets from "this is you"
to "this is your whole generation."

## A brief history

The oldest planets are the **seven you can see**. To the ancient world the sky
had seven "wandering stars" (the Greek *planetes* just means *wanderer*): the Sun,
the Moon, Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn, the lights that moved against
the fixed backdrop. Everything in classical astrology, all the rulerships, the
dignities, the sect doctrine, was built on these seven. For most of astrology's
history, the planet list *was* these seven.

Then the telescope changed the sky. **Uranus** was discovered in 1781,
**Neptune** in 1846, **Pluto** in 1930. And here's the part that should raise an
eyebrow: the meanings astrologers assigned to each map eerily onto the era of its
discovery. Uranus, planet of revolution and sudden upheaval, arrived between the
American and French revolutions. Neptune, planet of dream, illusion, and
dissolving boundaries, arrived amid Romanticism, spiritualism, and the rise of
anesthesia and photography. Pluto, planet of buried power and mass death and
transformation, arrived with the atomic age and the birth of depth psychology.
Whether you read that as meaningful or as astrologers reading the zeitgeist into
new toys, the correlation is real and worth knowing.

The asteroids came too. **Ceres** was found in 1801 (and, like Pluto, got
reclassified as a "dwarf planet" in 2006, which astrologers cheerfully ignored),
followed by the other main-belt bodies. **Chiron**, the first centaur, turned up
in 1977. The **trans-Neptunian objects** (Eris and the rest) are 21st-century
finds. Each new discovery is optional in a chart, which is exactly the tension
this chapter has to manage: more bodies mean more detail and more noise.

## The theory

### The four tiers

Sorted by speed, the ten planets fall into four natural groups. This is the map to
keep in your head.

| Planet | Tier | Governs |
|---|---|---|
| Sun ☉ | Luminary | Identity, will, vitality, the conscious self. |
| Moon ☽ | Luminary | Emotion, instinct, need, the private inner self. |
| Mercury ☿ | Personal | Mind, perception, language, learning. |
| Venus ♀ | Personal | Love, values, attraction, beauty, relating. |
| Mars ♂ | Personal | Drive, desire, assertion, anger, action. |
| Jupiter ♃ | Social | Expansion, meaning, growth, faith, excess. |
| Saturn ♄ | Social | Structure, limit, discipline, time, authority. |
| Uranus ♅ | Outer | Disruption, freedom, innovation, the sudden. |
| Neptune ♆ | Outer | Dissolution, dream, illusion, transcendence. |
| Pluto ♇ | Outer | Power, depth, death and rebirth, the buried. |

- The **luminaries** (Sun and Moon) are the two most important points in any
  chart: the conscious self and the emotional self. Half of astrology is the
  relationship between these two.
- The **personal planets** (Mercury, Venus, Mars) are fast and individual. They
  describe how *you specifically* think, love, and act. (Note that Mercury and
  Venus never wander far from the Sun; Mercury stays within ~28°, Venus within
  ~47°. Jung's Sun is in Leo, his Mercury and Venus both just behind it in Cancer,
  which is as far as they can get.)
- The **social planets** (Jupiter and Saturn) are the bridge. Slow enough to color
  a year-group, fast enough to still be personal, they govern how you meet the
  world of law, growth, and structure.
- The **outer planets** (Uranus, Neptune, Pluto) are generational. They sit in a
  sign for years, so they describe your *cohort* more than you. They become
  personal only when they touch a personal point (see "In practice" below).

### Motion: retrograde, stationary, out-of-bounds

A planet isn't just *where* it is but *how it's moving*, and three motion states
carry real weight.

**Retrograde** is the apparent-backward-motion from [chapter 00](./00_orientation.md).
A retrograde planet's function turns *inward*: it's rehearsed internally, worked
out privately, sometimes delayed or revisited before it flows outward. Jung's
**Saturn is retrograde**, which reads as an inner authority, a self-imposed
discipline answering to a private standard rather than an external one. (The lunar
nodes, below, are always retrograde by their mean motion, so don't read that as
significant for them; it's just how they move.)

**Stationary** is the sleeper. When a planet turns from direct to retrograde or
back, it slows to a near-standstill, and a near-stationary planet is *emphasized*,
its energy concentrated and insistent. Jung's **Mars is nearly stationary** (0.016°
a day, a crawl), which turns up the volume on it.

**Out-of-bounds** means a planet's declination has pushed past the Sun's maximum
(±23°27′), so it's literally outside the boundary the Sun ever travels. Such a
planet operates *off the leash*, outside the usual rules. Jung's **Mars is
out-of-bounds** at −27.9° declination, and stack that with its near-stationary
emphasis and you get the picture: a powerfully concentrated, rule-breaking drive
(Mars) aimed at the far philosophical horizon (Sagittarius). For the man who broke
with Freud and went looking for the psyche's outer edges, that is not a subtle
signature. Declination gets its own chapter ([10](./10_declination.md)); for now,
"out-of-bounds" means "unusually free of the normal limits."

### The points that aren't bodies

Some of the most-used chart factors aren't physical objects at all.

**The lunar nodes** are the two points where the Moon's orbit crosses the
ecliptic, and they're always opposite each other. The **North Node** (Rahu, in
Vedic astrology) marks a direction of growth, the unfamiliar thing you're here to
develop; the **South Node** (Ketu) marks the well-worn groove of the past, the
comfortable competence you're meant to grow *beyond*. Jung's axis runs **North
Node in Aries, South Node in Libra**: the pull of the past toward accommodation
and partnership (Libra), the growth edge toward standing alone and asserting a
self (Aries). A man who had to break from his mentor to become himself wore that
axis well.

**Black Moon Lilith** is the Moon's apogee, the empty focal point of its orbit
farthest from Earth. It's a modern favorite, associated with the raw, untamed,
refused-to-be-domesticated feminine and with what a person won't suppress. Use it,
but lightly; it's a sensitive point, not a heavyweight, and it comes in several
flavors (Mean, True, Osculating) that disagree by degrees.

### The extended cast (add judiciously)

Beyond the ten planets, Stellium offers a deep bench. The discipline is knowing
that more bodies is not more insight.

- **Asteroids** (Ceres, Pallas, Juno, Vesta, Hygiea): the main-belt bodies,
  often used to add texture around nurture (Ceres), wisdom and strategy (Pallas),
  partnership (Juno), and devotion (Vesta). Jung has all four clustered in Leo,
  with Vesta sitting right on his Sun (0.8°): devotion to a calling fused with
  identity, apt for a man who treated the psyche as a lifelong vocation.
- **Centaurs**, led by **Chiron**, the "wounded healer": the wound that can't be
  fully healed but becomes the source of your power to heal others. There is a
  quiet poetry in the fact that Chiron was discovered in 1977, sixteen years after
  the death of Jung, the twentieth century's great healer-of-psyches, who lived
  and died without ever seeing his own Chiron (in Aries, the wound around the
  raw right to exist).
- **Trans-Neptunian objects** (Eris, Sedna, Makemake, and more): the distant
  dwarf planets and bodies, still building an interpretive tradition.
- **Uranian / Hamburg School points** (Cupido, Hades, and the rest): hypothetical
  points used in a specific midpoint-based tradition covered in
  [chapter 18](./18_uranian_and_midpoints.md).

That's 37 selectable objects in all. The skill is restraint, which is the next
section.

## In practice

### Weight the classical seven first

A reading built on the seven visible planets, well understood, will be richer
than one drowning in forty bodies. Start there. The Sun-Moon relationship, the
condition of the personal planets, the placement of Jupiter and Saturn: that's the
spine. Everything else is optional seasoning.

### Outer planets matter when they get personal

An outer planet in its sign is generational background noise, *until* it lands on
an angle or aspects a personal planet, at which point the collective wave breaks
on the individual shore. Jung is the perfect illustration. His Neptune and Pluto
both sit in Taurus, standard for anyone born in the mid-1870s, so on their own
they say little about him. But his **Moon is also in Taurus**, wedged between them,
which threads his private emotional life directly into that generation's deep,
slow, transformative undertow. The generational became personal because it touched
his Moon. That's the test: does the outer planet connect to something that's
*his*?

The principle reaches all the way out to the slowest bodies in the chart. Eris,
which barely moves in a human lifetime, is pure generational wallpaper for
everyone born in the 1870s, parked in Pisces. But Jung's Eris makes a nearly exact
trine (0.1°) to his Mercury, and that hair-tight aspect drags it into the
personal: his mind (Mercury) running on Eris, the archetype of the excluded thing
that fights its way back to a rightful seat. For the man who built a psychology
around integrating the Shadow (the cast-out parts of the self), a generational
disruptor wired straight into his thinking is exactly the kind of hit worth
catching. The body's speed says generational; the tight aspect says *his*.

### Don't make asteroid soup

> [!CAUTION]
> Turning on every body at once produces a chart so crowded that nothing stands
> out. Add extras with a *question*, not by default: a nurture theme calls for
> Ceres, an old wound for Chiron. Otherwise, leave them off. A signal is only a
> signal against a quiet background.

### Read the motion flags as emphasis

Retrograde, stationary, and out-of-bounds are all ways a planet raises its hand.
When you're deciding what matters in a chart, a stationary or out-of-bounds planet
(like Jung's Mars) is asking for extra attention, even if it isn't the tightest
aspect or the most dignified placement.

## In Stellium

### The default cast, and reading a planet's motion

```python
from stellium import ChartBuilder

chart = ChartBuilder.from_notable("Carl Jung").calculate()

# The default set is the 10 planets, the lunar nodes, and Chiron.
for p in chart.get_planets():
    print(f"{p.name:9} {p.sign_position:22} speed {p.speed_longitude:+.3f}°/day")

mars = chart.get_object("Mars")
print(mars.is_retrograde)       # False
print(mars.is_out_of_bounds)    # True  <- declination past ±23°27'
print(round(mars.declination, 1), "declination")   # -27.9
print(round(mars.speed_longitude, 3), "°/day (near-stationary)")  # 0.016
```

Every `CelestialPosition` carries `speed_longitude` (negative means retrograde),
`is_retrograde`, `is_out_of_bounds`, and `declination`, so the motion states from
the theory section are all one attribute away.

### The luminaries and the nodes

```python
sun  = chart.get_object("Sun")
moon = chart.get_object("Moon")
print(sun.sign, moon.sign)                 # Leo Taurus

north = chart.get_object("True Node")      # aka "North Node"
south = chart.get_object("South Node")
print(north.sign, "/", south.sign)         # Aries / Libra
```

`"True Node"` is the default; pass a config with the Mean Node if you prefer the
smoothed version. Black Moon Lilith is `chart.get_object("Mean Apogee")` (alias
`"Black Moon Lilith"`) once you include the calculated points.

### Turning on the extended cast

```python
from stellium import ChartBuilder
from stellium.core.config import CalculationConfig

chart = (ChartBuilder.from_notable("Carl Jung")
    .with_config(CalculationConfig.comprehensive())  # asteroids + points (Lilith, etc.)
    .with_uranian()     # Cupido, Hades, Zeus, Kronos, ... (Hamburg School)
    .calculate())

for name in ("Ceres", "Chiron", "Cupido"):
    obj = chart.get_object(name)
    if obj:
        print(f"{name:8} {obj.sign_position}")
# Ceres    21°27' Leo
# Chiron   26°24' Aries
# Cupido   12°39' Gemini
```

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Ephemeris gotcha.** The main-belt asteroids, Chiron, and the Uranian points
> ship with Stellium, but the trans-Neptunian objects (Eris, Sedna, Makemake, and
> friends) need their ephemeris files downloaded first. Call `with_tnos()` without
> them and Stellium *warns and skips* those bodies rather than crashing, so
> `chart.get_object("Eris")` quietly comes back `None`. Download them once with
> `stellium ephemeris download-asteroid --tnos` and they'll resolve on every
> future chart. (Same bundled-vs-downloaded split as the
> [README's ephemeris section](../../../README.md#ephemeris-data-location).)

The full menu of all 37 objects (planets, nodes, points, asteroids, centaurs,
TNOs, Uranian hypotheticals) with glyphs and aliases lives in
[`docs/options_list.md`](../../options_list.md#celestial-objects-37-objects). Add
them deliberately, per the asteroid-soup warning above.
